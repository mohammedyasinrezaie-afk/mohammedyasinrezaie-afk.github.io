01 — Overview

What It Does

This is a fully custom-designed 192Wh portable power bank built from LiFePO₄ cells — the safest lithium chemistry available. It outputs up to 60W via USB-C and USB-A for phones and tablets, and provides raw 6.4V at up to 20A through an XT60 connector for higher-power applications.

A 0.91" OLED display driven by an ATtiny85 shows live voltage, current, power, and remaining energy. The modular mounting system allows attachments like a solar charge controller or LED light array to be added externally.

Final build — 3D-printed PLA enclosure with M3 hardware, OLED display, and XT60 high-current output port
02 — Problem

The Problem

Commercial power banks are misleading. Most advertise capacity in mAh — a meaningless figure without voltage — and deliver far less energy than claimed. A 25,000mAh Li-ion bank at 3.7V is only ~92Wh. Premium options like the UGreen 200W Nexode Pro offer just 90Wh for $120 AUD.

Beyond capacity, commercial power banks share three common weaknesses: they use Li-ion cells (less thermally stable than LiFePO₄), they can't be charged from a solar panel directly, and they offer no real-time energy monitoring. This build addresses all three — delivering 192Wh for ~$70 AUD total.

03 — Design Approach

Design Approach

The design was built around safety, capacity, and modularity. LiFePO₄ chemistry was chosen over Li-ion for its flat discharge curve, thermal stability, and longer cycle life. The 2S5P cell configuration balances voltage and capacity within the constraints of a common BMS.

04 — Technical Implementation

Technical Implementation

Battery pack: 10 cells wired in a 2S5P arrangement — two groups of five cells in parallel, then those groups in series. Each parallel group provides 6Ah at 3.2V; in series the pack outputs 6.4V nominal at 30Ah (192Wh). A 2S LiFePO₄ BMS handles cell balancing, overcurrent, and short-circuit protection.

Monitoring system: The ATtiny85 reads a 30A ACS712 current sensor and a resistor-divider voltage sensor at regular intervals. It integrates current over time to track watt-hours in and out, storing a running energy tally so it survives power cycles. Data is displayed on a 0.91" OLED via software I²C (bit-banging), with a momentary button toggling between display screens.

Power switch: A toggle switch isolates the entire system — including the ATtiny — preventing parasitic drain (~0.3W idle) which would otherwise deplete the pack in ~26 days.

06 — Challenges & Iteration

Challenges & Iteration

Case Iterations & Tight Tolerances

The enclosure went through multiple print iterations. Mounting positions were too tight in early versions, and one OLED display was cracked during a forced fit — corrected by adding clearance and test-fitting before final assembly.

ATtiny85 I²C Bit-Banging

The ATtiny85 lacks hardware I²C, requiring software bit-banging to drive the OLED. Getting reliable communication took significant debugging — timing and pull-up resistor values were critical.

Energy Counter Resetting

Early firmware reset the cumulative energy count on every power cycle. Fixed by storing the running tally in EEPROM so it persists across shutdowns.

Button Placement & Hole Diameters

Several button cutouts and port holes were undersized or misaligned across early iterations — corrected incrementally through measured reprints.
