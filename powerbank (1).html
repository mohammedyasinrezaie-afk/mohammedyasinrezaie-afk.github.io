01 — Overview

What It Does

This is a fully custom-designed 192Wh portable power bank built from LiFePO₄ cells — the safest and most cycle-stable lithium chemistry available. It delivers up to 60W via USB-C and USB-A for everyday devices, and provides raw 6.4V at up to 20A through an XT60 connector for high-power applications like solar charging systems or custom electronics.

A 0.91" OLED display driven by an ATtiny85 microcontroller shows live voltage, current draw, and cumulative energy — both in and out. The enclosure is 3D printed in PLA with M3 hardware and features modular mounting points for external attachments such as a solar charge controller or LED array.

Final build — 3D-printed PLA enclosure with integrated handle, OLED display, and XT60 high-current output port
02 — Problem

The Problem

Commercial power banks advertise capacity in mAh — a figure that is meaningless without voltage. A 25,000mAh Li-ion bank at 3.7V delivers only ~92Wh of actual energy. Even premium products underdeliver: the UGreen 200W Nexode Pro (25,000mAh) provides just 90Wh for $120 AUD.

Beyond capacity, off-the-shelf power banks share three key limitations: they use Li-ion chemistry (less thermally stable than LiFePO₄), they cannot be charged from a solar panel directly, and they offer no real-time energy data. This build solves all three — delivering 192Wh for ~$70 AUD, with solar input and live monitoring built in.

03 — Design Approach

Design Approach

Every design decision prioritised safety, real usable capacity, and modularity. LiFePO₄ chemistry was chosen for its flat discharge curve, thermal stability, and 2000+ cycle lifespan — far exceeding standard Li-ion.

04 — Technical Implementation

Technical Implementation

Battery pack: 10 LiFePO₄ 32700 cells (6Ah, 3.2V each) wired in a 2S5P configuration — two parallel groups of five cells connected in series. Each parallel group contributes 6Ah at 3.2V; in series the pack outputs 6.4V nominal at 30Ah (192Wh total). A dedicated 2S LiFePO₄ BMS handles cell balancing, overcurrent protection, and short-circuit cutoff.

Monitoring system: The ATtiny85 samples an ACS712 hall-effect current sensor (30A range) and a resistor-divider voltage sensor at regular intervals. It integrates current over time (∫I·dt) to track watt-hours in and out, storing the running tally in EEPROM so values survive power cycles. A momentary push button cycles through display screens on the 0.91" OLED.

Power isolation: A toggle switch disconnects the entire system including the ATtiny85, preventing ~0.3W of parasitic idle drain that would otherwise deplete the pack in approximately 26 days. It also acts as an isolation switch on the XT60 connector for safety.

Energy tracking: The ATtiny85 integrates current readings over time to calculate watt-hours consumed and charged. This value is written to EEPROM and persists across power cycles — giving an accurate, cumulative state-of-charge readout on the display at all times.

05 — Testing & Results

Testing & Results

192Wh Total usable capacity
35W Confirmed solar charge rate
20A XT60 output current at 6.4V
~$70 Total build cost (AUD)

Capacity vs. commercial: The UGreen 200W Nexode Pro delivers 90Wh for $120 AUD. This build delivers 192Wh — more than double — for $70 AUD, while adding solar charging and live energy monitoring that no commercial bank provides at any price point.

Solar test: A 100W panel (~20V open circuit) stepped down via a buck converter to 7.3V delivered a confirmed 35W charge rate into the pack — fully viable for off-grid use.

Idle drain: With the isolation switch open, measured idle consumption is 0W. With the switch closed and ATtiny85 active, idle draw is ~0.3W — equivalent to a full drain in ~26 days, motivating the switch design.

06 — Challenges & Iteration

Challenges & Iteration

Enclosure Iterations & Tight Fits

The 3D-printed case went through multiple redesigns. Early versions had mounting positions that were too tight — one OLED display was cracked during a forced fit. Resolved by adding clearance tolerances to the CAD model and test-fitting components before final assembly.

ATtiny85 Software I²C (Bit-Banging)

The ATtiny85 has no hardware I²C peripheral, requiring a software bit-bang implementation to drive the OLED. Getting reliable communication required careful tuning of timing delays and pull-up resistor values — a deep dive into low-level embedded communication protocols.

Energy Counter Resetting on Power Cycle

Early firmware lost the cumulative energy count every time the unit was powered off. Fixed by writing the running tally to EEPROM at regular intervals, allowing the display to resume from the last recorded value on startup.

Button & Port Placement

Several button cutouts and connector holes were misaligned or undersized across early print iterations. Corrected incrementally through careful measurement and targeted reprints — reinforcing the importance of dimensioned CAD tolerances.
