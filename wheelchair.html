01 — Overview

What It Does

This prototype is a scaled electric wheelchair demonstrating core mobility mechanics — differential steering via independent motor control, analog joystick input, and load-bearing capability. The joystick maps directly to motor speed and direction through an Arduino and H-bridge shield, giving smooth proportional control.

The project was motivated by the high cost of commercial mobility devices in Australia and aimed to validate that reliable motorised control could be achieved at a fraction of the price using accessible components.

02 — Problem

The Problem

Commercial electric wheelchairs in Australia cost between $2,000 and $18,000, placing them out of reach for many users without subsidy. The goal was to explore how far a low-cost prototype could go — specifically whether off-the-shelf microcontroller components could deliver reliable differential steering while carrying a meaningful load, within tight power and budget constraints.

03 — Design Approach

Design Approach

The design prioritised simplicity and testability — every component was chosen to be easily replaceable and measurable during iteration.

🧠

Arduino MCU

Reads analog joystick X/Y axes and maps them to differential motor speed via PWM output.

🔁

H-Bridge Shield

Motor shield handles the current demands of the drive motors, protecting the Arduino from back-EMF and overload.

⚙️

Modified Servos

Standard servos modified for continuous rotation — providing ~1.8 kg·cm stall torque at low cost.

🪵

Laser-Cut Chassis

Precise flat-pack chassis cut from sheet material — rigid, lightweight, and reproducible.

04 — Technical Implementation

Technical Implementation

Steering logic: The joystick outputs two analog voltages (X and Y axes). The Arduino maps these to left and right motor speeds — pushing forward drives both motors equally, while deflecting left slows the left motor and speeds up the right, producing a smooth arc turn. Full lateral deflection pivots the chassis in place.

Motor selection: Four modified continuous-rotation servo motors are used — two per side. Servos were chosen over plain DC motors for their built-in gearbox (higher torque) and ease of control via standard PWM signals without a separate encoder.

Torque calculation: Original 5V DC motors stalled under load and drew excessive current, risking the Arduino's voltage regulator. Modified servos at 1.8 kg·cm stall torque resolved both issues — providing headroom above the 1 kg test load while drawing current within the shield's rated limit.

05 — Testing & Results

Testing & Results

1 kg Load carried successfully (900mL water bottle)
1.8 kg·cm stall torque per motor
✓ Differential steering maintained under full load

The prototype met its core objective — carrying a 1 kg load while maintaining responsive joystick-controlled differential steering. Turning radius was smooth and consistent. The switch from DC motors to modified servos was the critical change that made load-bearing possible within the 9V power budget.

06 — Challenges & Iteration

Challenges & Iteration

Insufficient Motor Torque

Initial 5V DC motors couldn't move the chassis under load and drew current that threatened the Arduino's regulator. Replaced with modified continuous-rotation servos (~1.8 kg·cm) — resolved both torque and current issues.

Excessive Current Draw

Original motors pulled more current than the motor shield could safely supply under stall conditions. The servo swap reduced peak current draw and kept the system within safe operating limits.

Wheel Traction

Smooth wheels slipped on the test surface under load. Added rubber grip material around the wheel circumference to improve friction without adding weight.

07 — Future Improvements

Future Improvements

⚡

Geared DC Motors

Replace modified servos with proper geared DC motors for higher efficiency, better speed control, and longer runtime.

🔋

18650 Battery Pack

Upgrade from 9V to a dual 18650 Li-ion pack for higher capacity, stable voltage under load, and rechargeability.

📡

Bluetooth Control

Add a Bluetooth module so the wheelchair can be controlled from a smartphone — removing the need for a wired joystick.

🏗️

Full Enclosure

Design a protective shell over the electronics and a more refined chassis for durability and a closer resemblance to a real wheelchair form factor.
